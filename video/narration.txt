Gather close, and I will tell you of Cipherfell, a quiet village where folk kept their secrets on a handshake.

But the Baron of Thornmoor coveted it, and by night his agents slipped through the gate, wearing borrowed names and forged seals. So the Duke named a young Warden, with a single rule. Trust nothing you cannot prove.

To unmask the stranger at the gate, that a face, and a scar, cannot be borrowed.

To keep the Duke's letters safe, that the secret lives in the key, never in the message.

To catch a thief in a locked granary, that the fewer keys you hand out, the fewer hands can betray you.

To stop a baron who knew too much, that a hundred harmless whispers, gathered together, become a secret.

To refuse a summons in the Duke's own name, for a message cannot vouch for itself.

To catch a doctored ledger, that a broken seal betrays a hidden change.

And when he set the records ablaze, that what is kept in two places cannot be burned away.

No swords. No machines. Only a careful eye, and the quiet craft of keeping what matters safe. Cipherfell. The Warden's Eye.
